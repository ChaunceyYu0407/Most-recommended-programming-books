What I Learned Writing Amazon’s Best-Selling Python Book

Cory Althoff
Follow
Feb 23, 2017 · 6 min read

It all started in the backseat of a taxi in Bali. I had just left my job as a software engineer in Silicon Valley and, along with my girlfriend, bought a one-way ticket to Southeast Asia. As the taxi drove through the narrow streets of Seminyak in the rain, I had an idea. Back home, people were always asking me about my experience as software engineer. It is not unusual to be a software engineer in Silicon Valley, but I was different than many of my peers because I do not have a CS degree—I majored in political science.

My idea was to write a book called The Self-Taught Programmer. The book would not only be about programming but everything I learned to get hired as a software engineer. I told my girlfriend what I was thinking and let her know

“This isn’t going to be one of those things I talk about and don’t follow through with. I am going to do this.”

Start Writing Today

If you have an idea for a book, start immediately. The more time you wait, the more likely it will remain an idea. I started writing day one. I typed furiously into Google Docs, like everyone in the world was watching me, and expecting I wouldn’t follow through. The next day I hired someone to design a cover, which gave me momentum because it made the project feel real.

Get Inspired

We stayed in Bali for a few more weeks. It was like another planet. The beaches were beautiful. And the humidity! Nothing makes you feel alive like 90% humidity. We rented a scooter and spent hours driving around the island from one hostel to the next.

I worked on my book in bed when I woke up. I worked on my book in coffee shops. And I worked on my book before I went to sleep. It was hard to get me to do anything else. My new environment fueled my creativity. You don’t need to travel across the world to find inspiration, but find a way to get out of your comfort zone.

Don’t Wait to Market

When I returned to reality, I started to have doubts. I began to think I was wasting my time because no one would read my book. I decided to test the demand for my product before I committed any more time to it. I ran a Facebook ad with the title of my book and a picture of the cover. I wrote:

“Coming Soon.”

The ad surpassed my expectations. It generated hundreds of likes, shares, and comments. My doubts subsided, and I knew I had a product people would buy. I built a website for my book, started a newsletter, and continued to run Facebook ads while I was writing. The success of my ad taught me a valuable lesson: you should not write a book and then market it—you should market it while you write.

Set a Deadline

I didn’t use to believe in deadlines. Why would I need a deadline for my book? I was working on it every day, and I told myself,

“I will finish it when I finish it.”

Regardless, I ended up having to set a deadline so I could accept pre-orders on Amazon. I picked the day after Christmas because people would have brand new iPads and Kindles, and they would be looking for something to read. In hindsight, I should have picked January 1st. People like new, and now my book’s publication date is in 2016 making it seem older than it is.

Once I could accept pre-orders, I got back to writing. Four weeks left. Three weeks left. Two weeks left. I wasn’t close to finished, and I could tell by the pace I was writing at I wasn’t going to finish on time.

I started deleting everything that wasn’t necessary. I deleted chapters that were almost finished. I removed entire sections of the book. If I didn’t have a deadline, I would have kept writing forever. I would never have ever finished because there was always something new I wanted to add. In the end, the tough choices my deadline forced me to make not only improved my book but saved the project.

Focus on Quality

With one week left, I had to stop writing. I didn’t know how to create a Kindle book, or how to publish a paperback version, and I needed time to figure it out.

The Self-Taught Programmer has over 400 programming examples. The lines of code can’t wrap when a screen changes size, and I wanted the coding examples to be color coated with line numbers, which most programming books do not have. I ended up pulling four all nighters formatting the book. I worked thirty hours straight writing software to help. I also learned how to create an index. It was terrible, but in the end, it was worth it because I love the way the formatting turned out.

If you are self-publishing, there is no reason your book cannot look better than a traditionally published project. Focus on quality. Don’t skip details like a copyright page or an index (if you are writing non-fiction). Study traditionally published books, find their weaknesses and make them your strengths.

Form a Team

When I used to think about writing, I would imagine Hemingway tapping on a typewriter and sipping scotch alone in a house in Cuba. Now I think of writing as a team sport. I owe the success my book has achieved so far to the outstanding team of people that helped me with it.

Five people edited my book, and all of them improved it. One of my friends spent two days in a coffee shop with me reading every sentence out loud and making improvements.

The excellent feedback I received made my book 10x better than it would have been if I worked on it alone.

Don’t Forget to Test

I took the same approach to testing my book as I did marketing it—I didn’t wait to start. I used my email newsletter to put together a list of 200 beta readers. I sent the book out in real time to them. I divided the book into six sections, and after I finished each section, I would send it to my beta readers for feedback.

I can’t thank the people that beta read my book enough. Not only did they catch errors and provide excellent feedback, but they also gave me encouragement as well. Nothing will motivate you to write more than a group of people excited about your book and asking for more chapters.

The Result

One year later, I am thrilled that The Self-Taught Programmer is not only available on Amazon, but as of this writing, it is the number one book in several categories, including Python.

I am excited people are reading my book. More importantly, I am thrilled people are using my book to learn to program.

If you’ve always wanted to learn to program or write a book, now is the time to start. I hope you can use my experience to avoid the mistakes I made.

As Jocko Willink likes to say, “It’s time to get after it.”
